# Head of Business Development & Operations — Strategic Daily Routine

## Purpose

This role exists to turn uncertainty into evidence, pipeline, and financial control.

Until the company has an iron-clad business idea, your job is not to protect ideas emotionally. Your job is to run a repeatable operating loop that produces four outputs every week:

- qualified demand signals
- verified market intelligence
- disciplined unit economics and cash visibility
- a clearer go, refine, park, or kill decision on the current business idea

This routine is designed to keep you commercially active while making business idea vetting a standing part of the job rather than a side project.

---

## Core Role Outcomes

You own the commercial and operational truth layer of the company.

Your success is measured by whether you can:

1. Create and progress a real demand pipeline with the highest-value target segment for the current thesis.
2. Extract real pain points from the market faster than competitors do.
3. Keep the company financially literate and operationally disciplined.
4. Turn market evidence into a sharper business thesis every week.

## Daily Non-Negotiables

Every working day should produce the following minimum outputs:

- 10-20 new target entities, users, buyers, counterparties, or potential partners identified
- 15-25 outbound touches, or 5-10 highly personalised messages for high-value targets
- 2 external conversations moved forward: sales call, discovery interview, partner call, or warm follow-up
- 1 new market insight logged: pain point, buying trigger, objection, pricing signal, or competitor weakness
- 1 concrete advance on the active business thesis
- 1 financial or operational review touchpoint, even if brief

If a day ends without pipeline movement, market learning, thesis progress, and financial visibility, it was busy but not strategic.

## Strategic Daily Schedule

### 08:00-08:30 — Command Review

- Review cash position, runway, deal pipeline, calendar, open proposals, and urgent follow-ups.
- Set one commercial priority for the day and one thesis priority for the day.
- Identify the three targets, partners, or opportunities that matter most.

**Rule**: the thesis priority is not the random idea you thought of this morning. It is the current lead hypothesis for the week. New ideas should be captured in a backlog and only promoted if the evidence looks materially stronger.

**Output**: a short daily priority list with one revenue target and one research target.

### 08:30-10:00 — Business Thesis Block

Protect this block. This is where idea vetting happens.

Work on one active thesis at a time using:

- `Business/RESEARCH/directives/business-thesis.md`
- `Business/RESEARCH/resources/business-thesis-template.md`

The active thesis is the current best hypothesis, not necessarily a business-software idea. It can target businesses, traders, investors, funds, operators, retail participants, or another segment if the evidence supports it.

Rotate through the thesis in a hard evidence sequence:

1. Problem definition and customer pain
2. Target user or buyer profile and current workaround
3. Competitor map and basis of competition
4. Market size and willingness-to-pay logic
5. Moat, substitution, and integration stress tests
6. Unit economics and operational feasibility
7. Clear recommendation: go, refine, park, or kill

**Rule**: thesis work must end with a decision signal, not just more notes.

**Conversation matching rule**: talk to the people closest to the active thesis. If the thesis is trading-related, that can mean traders, analysts, desk operators, brokers, educators, risk stakeholders, or funds rather than standard business buyers.

### 10:00-11:30 — Outreach and Pipeline Creation

- Build target lists for the current thesis: businesses, traders, funds, operators, partners, or another relevant segment.
- Run focused outreach by segment, problem, and trigger event.
- Prioritise quality over sheer volume when the account value is high.
- Ask for meetings, referrals, or a defined next step. Do not settle for vague interest.

**Output**: new conversations opened or existing conversations progressed.

### 11:30-12:30 — Discovery and Commercial Conversations

- Run demos, discovery calls, partner calls, or short customer interviews.
- Probe for urgency, money at stake, workflow friction, trust requirements, switching friction, and proof requirements.
- Listen for the exact language the target segment uses to describe the pain.

**Output**: stronger qualification, better positioning insight, or a committed next action.

### 13:30-14:30 — Competitor and Market Intelligence

- Review one or two competitors in depth: homepage, pricing, case studies, onboarding flow, integrations, customer proof, and public complaints.
- Track what the category over-promises and where it still fails operationally.
- Log one product implication and one messaging implication.

**Output**: at least one actionable market insight added to the thesis or GTM notes.

### 14:30-15:15 — Financial and Operating Control

- Update a lightweight operating dashboard.
- Review cash balance, monthly burn, runway, pipeline value, expected conversion, and likely near-term revenue.
- Re-check unit economics assumptions on the current idea or offer.
- Flag any mismatch between the story you are telling the market and the economics underneath it.

**Output**: numbers are current enough to support decisions.

### 15:15-16:30 — Deal Progression and Partnerships

- Send recaps, proposals, and next-step emails.
- Progress one strategic partnership thread each day when possible.
- Ask for introductions from warm contacts.
- Kill or downgrade stale opportunities instead of carrying false pipeline.

**Output**: cleaner pipeline and stronger deal momentum.

### 16:30-17:00 — Daily Debrief

- Log what moved, what blocked, what you learned, and what changes tomorrow.
- Record one insight for product direction and one insight for go-to-market positioning.
- Update the thesis with any new evidence while it is still fresh.

**Output**: a complete daily record, not a memory-based reconstruction later.

## Weekly Thesis Vetting Loop

The active business thesis should move on a five-day loop so idea vetting compounds every week.

| Day | Thesis focus | Required output |
|-----|--------------|-----------------|
| Monday | Scope the idea, define the target segment, define the pain, set the thesis questions | One-page problem framing |
| Tuesday | Competitor map, pricing review, category analysis | Comparison table and basis-of-competition notes |
| Wednesday | Market calls, pain-point validation, willingness-to-pay signals | Interview notes and objection patterns |
| Thursday | Unit economics, market size, moat score, operational feasibility | First-pass business case and risk list |
| Friday | Write the recommendation and decide go, refine, park, or kill | Weekly thesis memo |

If the idea survives Friday, the next week deepens the strongest unanswered question. If it fails Friday, archive it cleanly and move on.

## Daily KPI Scoreboard

Track these daily. Weekly aggregation is useful, but the discipline has to be daily.

| Metric | Daily target |
|--------|--------------|
| New target entities or personas added | 10-20 |
| Outbound touches | 15-25 |
| Positive replies or warm continuations | 3+ |
| Calls held | 2+ |
| Opportunities moved to next stage | 1-3 |
| Competitor or market insights logged | 1+ |
| Thesis progress units completed | 1+ |
| Financial dashboard reviewed | 1 |

Use these targets as a forcing function, not vanity metrics. A smaller number of high-quality actions beats hollow volume.

---

## Operating Rules

1. Do not spend the day researching without speaking to the market.
2. Do not spend the day selling without updating the thesis.
3. Do not let new ideas interrupt the active thesis without a written reason to switch.
4. Do not trust pipeline numbers that have no next step and no owner.
5. Do not describe an idea as strong if the unit economics or moat logic are weak.
6. Do not confuse activity with evidence.

## Practical Rhythm For This Workspace

Use the departments in this workspace as the operating system for the role:

- **RESEARCH** for business thesis creation, competitor analysis, and market stress-testing.
- **PRODUCT** for customer discovery, pain-point logging, and shaping what should be built.
- **ORCHESTRATOR** for daily rhythm, priority control, and weekly review discipline.

The role works when these three lanes stay connected:

- market conversations create signals
- research turns signals into structured judgement
- operations turns judgement into disciplined action

## End-Of-Week Review Questions

Answer these every Friday:

1. What did the target market actually care about this week?
2. Which pain point showed repeated urgency?
3. Which competitor looked stronger than expected, and why?
4. Which assumption in the current thesis got weaker?
5. Does the current idea deserve deeper investment next week?
6. Is the company closer to revenue, or just more informed?

If you cannot answer these clearly, the routine is being performed mechanically instead of strategically.
